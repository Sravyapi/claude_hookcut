Priya started her podcast in 2022 as a passion project — long, unscripted conversations about psychology, relationships, and modern Indian life. By early 2025, she had a dedicated listener base of around 12,000 people and was publishing 60–90 minute episodes every two weeks.

"My podcast was growing, but slowly," she says. "I knew YouTube Shorts was where I needed to be — I just couldn't figure out how to make podcast clips that actually worked."

The problem: podcast conversations aren't structured like YouTube videos. There's no clear beginning or end to any single point. There's crosstalk, tangents, and the kind of natural flow that makes great audio but terrible Shorts.

"Every time I tried to clip it, I'd end up with something that started mid-sentence and ended at a weird place. Or I'd pick a moment that made sense to me as a listener but had no hook for someone who'd never heard the podcast."

---

## The Language Factor

Priya's podcast is Hinglish — a natural mix of Hindi and English that reflects how many Indians actually talk. This created an additional challenge: most clipping tools have weak support for multilingual content.

"I tried one of the popular AI clippers, and it didn't understand my content at all. It was scoring segments based on English-only analysis and completely missing the Hindi portions — which are often the most emotionally resonant parts."

She ran three episodes through HookCut in February 2025.

---

## What HookCut Found

The first episode HookCut analyzed was a 75-minute conversation about loneliness in young professionals — a topic Priya knew resonated with her audience but had never successfully clipped.

HookCut surfaced a 52-second moment at 31:14 in the episode. The clip opened with Priya in mid-conversation: *"Yaar, the thing nobody talks about is — you can be surrounded by people and still feel completely invisible. That's not loneliness. That's something else."*

"When I saw that in isolation, I immediately understood why it worked," she says. "It names a specific experience. It creates a label for something people feel but don't have words for. And it's in natural Hinglish — it sounds like how we actually talk."

The clip scored 88 out of 100. The second-highest scoring clip from the same episode was a 41-second moment from the 58-minute mark — another segment buried deep in the conversation that Priya hadn't considered clipping.

---

## Results Over 90 Days

Priya posted the two clips from that first episode. Combined, they reached 890,000 views in 30 days.

Over the following 90 days, she ran 12 more podcast episodes through HookCut, posting 2–3 Shorts per episode. Her podcast listener count went from 12,000 to 62,000 — a gain of 50,000 listeners in 90 days.

"The Shorts were working as trailers," she says. "People would watch a clip, feel something, and then go find the full episode. The click-through from Shorts to the podcast was something I hadn't expected."

Her YouTube channel crossed 22,000 subscribers in the same period — up from 3,200 before she started using HookCut.

---

## The Pattern That Made It Work

Looking at the clips that performed best, Priya noticed a consistent pattern: the ones that went viral were almost always from the middle of the episode, not the beginning or end.

"The first 10 minutes of any podcast episode is warmup — catching up, setup, light banter. That's the filler. The real conversation starts later. HookCut kept finding moments from the 30–50 minute mark, which is exactly where the episode got real."

She also noticed that the best hooks from her podcast shared a specific structure: they named an experience or feeling, then reframed it in a way the listener hadn't considered.

"It's not enough to just say something true. The hook has to say something true in a way the viewer didn't expect. That's what HookCut was identifying — not just the emotional moments, but the ones with a reframe built in."

---

## What She Does Differently Now

Priya now runs every episode through HookCut within 24 hours of publishing. She posts 2 Shorts per episode — the highest and second-highest scoring hooks.

"I don't clip manually anymore," she says. "I trust the tool to find the moments. My job is to make sure the conversation is real. HookCut's job is to find where it gets real enough to clip."

She also changed how she closes episodes. After seeing which moments HookCut consistently scored highest, she started deliberately creating more of those moments — the specific, reframing, emotionally honest statements that stop scrollers.

"The tool made me a better podcast host," she says. "That wasn't what I expected."
