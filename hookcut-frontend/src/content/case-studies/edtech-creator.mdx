Kiran teaches physics and mathematics on YouTube in a mix of Malayalam and English, serving students preparing for competitive exams in Kerala. He started his channel in 2021 as a supplement to his offline coaching work and built a following of 67,000 subscribers — but his Shorts were consistently underperforming.

"I knew my content was good," he says. "My long-form videos had strong watch time — students were watching 45-minute videos on electrostatics. But my Shorts would get 1,000 views and 12 seconds of watch time. Nobody was staying."

The low watch time was the critical signal. It told Kiran not that his content was wrong, but that his openings weren't working. If viewers weren't staying past 12 seconds, the hook — or lack of one — was the problem.

---

## The Educational Content Challenge

Educational Shorts have a structural disadvantage: they often require context before the insight lands. A mathematics trick is only impressive once you understand the problem it solves. A physics concept only surprises you if you knew the intuitive answer first.

"The temptation with educational content is to start with context," Kiran says. "You want to set up the problem before you show the solution. But that's the opposite of what works in Shorts."

He had tried restructuring his clips — leading with the answer, then showing the work. But something wasn't connecting. The clips still felt slow.

---

## Running the Videos Through HookCut

In March 2025, Kiran ran six of his recent videos through HookCut. He expected it to surface the most visually interesting moments — calculations appearing on the board, dramatic "aha" moments.

What it found instead were opening lines — specific sentences from his explanations that contained a question or a contradiction.

"The highest-scoring clip was from a video about optics," he says. "It started with me saying: 'If light travels in straight lines — and it does — then explain why you can see around corners. You do it every day. Here's what's actually happening.'"

That clip scored 94. At the time Kiran was recording, he hadn't thought of it as a hook — it was just a way to make the concept concrete.

"But as a clip, it's a perfect hook. It creates a question the viewer can't answer immediately. It challenges something they thought they knew. And it promises an explanation in the same breath."

---

## The Results

Kiran posted the clip from the optics video. It reached 2.3 million views with an average watch duration of 38 seconds — against his previous Short average of 12 seconds. That's a 3.2x improvement on a single clip.

Over the following four months, Kiran ran 22 videos through HookCut and posted 44 Shorts. His Shorts average watch duration went from 12 seconds to 50 seconds — a 4.2x improvement. His channel subscriber count grew by 31,000 in the same period.

The effect on his long-form videos was equally significant. The Shorts that performed best drove viewers to the source video, which saw completion rates improve from 34% to 61% — a metric Kiran attributes to better-matched audience intent.

"The people coming from the Shorts already knew what the video was about," he says. "The hook pre-qualified them. They weren't randomly landing on my channel — they came because they saw the exact question the video answers."

---

## Malayalam + English: The Language Factor

Kiran's content is multilingual — he often code-switches between Malayalam and English mid-sentence, the way teachers naturally do in Kerala. This created a concern when he first tried AI tools: would they handle mixed-language content?

"HookCut analyzed the content correctly," he says. "It wasn't treating the Malayalam portions as noise or errors. The scoring was reflecting what was actually happening in the conversation, not just the English words."

Two of his five highest-performing Shorts were clips where the hook line was entirely in Malayalam — and those performed at least as well as the English-primary clips.

---

## What Changed in His Production Approach

The most significant change Kiran made wasn't to his content — it was to how he opens explanations.

"I started asking myself, before I explain anything: what's the question this raises? What's the contradiction? What's the thing the student thinks they know that this is going to prove wrong?"

He now starts his videos by stating the contradiction or the question before the explanation. "It makes better educational content anyway," he says. "Leading with the problem forces you to be clear about what you're actually teaching."

His Shorts posting schedule is now consistent: two Shorts per long-form video, posted within 48 hours of the video going live. Average views per Short: 340,000.

"I used to think Shorts were a different product from long-form content," he says. "Now I think of them as the trailer. The job of the Short is to create the question. The job of the long video is to answer it."
