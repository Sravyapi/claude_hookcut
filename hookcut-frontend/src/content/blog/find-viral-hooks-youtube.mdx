Every long-form YouTube video contains at least one moment worth posting as a Short. Most creators never find it. Here's how to change that.

---

## What makes a moment a "hook"?

Before you can find hooks, you need to know what you're looking for.

A hook is a specific moment in your video where a viewer's attention shifts from passive to engaged. It's the frame where someone who was about to scroll past stops — and watches.

Hooks share common structural features:

- **They create a gap between what the viewer knows and what they want to know** (curiosity gap)
- **They present something unexpected or counterintuitive** (pattern interrupt)
- **They articulate a pain the viewer recognizes** (pain escalation)
- **They make a claim the viewer isn't sure is true** (contrarian claim)
- **They establish social proof at a surprising scale** (social proof)

Notice what's not on the list: production quality, length, entertainment value. Hooks are about *information gaps*, not production value.

---

## The 18 hook types

Understanding hook type classification helps you identify and categorize moments as you review your content.

**Attention Hooks** (create initial stop):
- Curiosity Gap: "Nobody tells you this about..."
- Pattern Interrupt: A visual or verbal non-sequitur that breaks the viewer's autopilot
- Shock Statistic: "94% of creators get this wrong..."
- Contrarian Claim: "Everyone is wrong about [common belief]"

**Engagement Hooks** (maintain attention once stopped):
- Pain Escalation: Articulates a specific problem with increasing precision
- Transformation: Before-and-after framing that implies a path
- Social Proof: "I've done this with 10,000 creators and..."
- Expert Positioning: Claims credibility from unexpected experience

**Retention Hooks** (keep viewers watching):
- Open Loop: "I'll tell you exactly how at the end, but first..."
- Cliffhanger: Implies a reveal that hasn't happened yet
- Stakes Raising: "If you get this wrong, here's what happens..."
- Mystery Setup: A question posed but not yet answered

**Conversion Hooks** (drive specific action):
- Offer Bridge: Connects problem to specific solution
- Proof Point: Demonstrates result before claiming it
- Time Sensitivity: Creates urgency without being dishonest
- Comparison: Frames against a known alternative

The most powerful Shorts often combine hook types — what's called a **composite hook**. A shock statistic that also creates a curiosity gap performs better than either alone.

## Manual method: how to find hooks in your own video

If you want to find hooks without any tools, here's a 4-step process:

### Step 1: Read your transcript, not watch your video

Watching your own video is slow and you'll be distracted by production choices. Export or copy your video transcript (YouTube auto-generates one for most videos). Read it as text.

As you read, highlight every sentence where:
- You make a claim the reader might question
- You use a specific number or statistic
- You imply something but don't immediately explain it
- You describe a problem in a specific, recognizable way

### Step 2: Find the tension peak

For each highlighted section, ask: *what would a viewer who doesn't know what happens next be thinking right now?*

If the answer is "nothing — this is a transition sentence", skip it. If the answer is "wait, is that actually true?" or "I need to know how this resolves", that's a hook candidate.

### Step 3: Find the start point, not the end

Most creators start their clips too late. The best starting point is usually 2–4 seconds before the hook line itself — the setup that gives the hook its impact.

For a curiosity gap hook, the start is often a declarative statement that hints at the gap. For a shock statistic, it's the framing context that makes the statistic surprising.

### Step 4: Find the end point

End the clip at the resolution of the emotional tension — not at the end of the grammatical sentence. If the hook creates a curiosity gap, end just after the gap begins to close. If it creates an open loop, end before the loop closes — let the viewer's rewatch rate do the work.

## The problem with manual review

Manual transcript review takes time. For a 30-minute video, expect to spend 45–90 minutes on careful review, clip selection, and trim decisions. For a podcast episode of 90 minutes or more, this can take half a day.

More importantly, manual review is biased. Creators tend to select moments they *like* — not moments with the highest structural engagement potential. The section you're most proud of and the section most likely to stop a scroll are often different sections.

## AI-assisted hook detection

AI-assisted tools analyze the full transcript and identify hook candidates based on structural and semantic patterns — the same patterns that experienced creators look for manually, but applied consistently across the entire video in seconds.

The key question when evaluating any AI hook tool: **does it tell you *why* a moment was chosen?**

A score without an explanation is just a black box. The explanation is what helps you learn — and what lets you override the AI when it misses context that only you know (inside jokes, audience-specific references, callbacks to previous videos).

When reviewing AI-identified hooks, ask:
- What hook type is this? Does the classification feel accurate?
- What would a first-time viewer's reaction be to this moment?
- Is there a better starting point 2–4 seconds earlier?
- Does the end point create an open loop or close too neatly?

## Common mistakes when selecting hooks

**Selecting the moment you're most proud of, not the most stop-worthy moment.** These are often different sections. Your most polished explanation is rarely your most compelling hook — raw, surprising, or counterintuitive moments usually are.

**Starting too late.** The hook is the transition into engagement. The setup before the hook is often more important than the hook line itself.

**Ending too neatly.** Shorts that resolve their own tension completely have lower rewatch rates. Leave something unresolved to pull viewers back.

**Ignoring the thumbnail.** The hook moment determines your thumbnail. If you can't extract a single frame from the moment that works as a thumbnail, reconsider whether it's actually a strong visual hook.

**Prioritizing length.** Thirty-second Shorts often outperform 90-second Shorts with the same hook. Always cut to the minimum length that preserves the full emotional arc.
